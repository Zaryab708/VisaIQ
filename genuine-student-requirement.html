Section 1

What is the Genuine Student Requirement?

The Genuine Student (GS) requirement applies to all student visa (subclass 500) applications lodged on or after 23 March 2024. It replaced the old Genuine Temporary Entrant (GTE) requirement. Applications lodged before that date continue to be assessed under GTE.

GS vs GTE — what actually changed
NameGenuine Temporary Entrant (GTE) — before 23 March 2024  →  Genuine Student (GS) — from 23 March 2024
PR plansGTE: Future PR intentions could count against you  →  GS: PR plans are explicitly allowed. DHA acknowledges genuine students may later apply for PR and this does not count against you.
FormatGTE was typically a separate attached document  →  GS answers are entered directly into the ImmiAccount online form. 150 words per question. DHA prefers in-form answers over attached statements.
FocusGTE focused on temporary stay intent  →  GS focuses on genuine study intent and whether the course makes sense for your background and goals.
Who it applies toAll student visa (subclass 500) applicants — both offshore and onshore. There is an additional question for those who have held a student visa before or are applying from a non-student visa.
Legal basisMinisterial Direction No. 106 (2024) — published by the Department of Home Affairs. This is the primary document DHA officers use when assessing your application.
The most important thing to know about GS: Under the old GTE, mentioning that you might apply for PR one day was risky. Under the new GS, this is explicitly fine. You can be honest about wanting a 485 post-study work visa, wanting to explore PR options, or hoping to stay long-term. None of that disqualifies you. What matters is whether your study intention is genuine.
Section 2

The 5 things DHA actually assesses

Under Ministerial Direction No. 106, DHA assesses the GS criterion by examining five specific areas. Understanding exactly what each area involves is the difference between a strong application and a refusal.

1. Your current circumstances and ties to home country

DHA looks at whether you have genuine connections to your home country that make it plausible you will comply with visa conditions. This includes family ties, employment, economic circumstances, community connections, and property ownership.

Family tiesParents, siblings, spouse, children remaining in home country. The stronger these ties, the stronger your case. A married person with children at home has very strong ties. A single person with no family connections has weaker ties and needs to compensate elsewhere.
Employment tiesA job you are taking leave from to study, a business you own, professional licences that only work in your home country. Evidence of a position you will return to is powerful.
Economic circumstancesDHA wants to understand that you have genuine financial capacity — not that you are desperate to leave for economic reasons. Bank statements, income tax returns, and employment documents for the 12 months before application are all relevant.
Property and assetsProperty ownership, land, investments in your home country all indicate genuine connections. Include evidence if you have it.
Similar course available locallyIf your chosen course is available in your home country, you must explain why you are coming to Australia specifically. Lack of explanation here is a common refusal ground.
2. Why this course with this provider in Australia

This is the most scrutinised question. DHA checks whether your course choice makes logical sense given your background and goals. A generic answer here is the single most common reason for refusal.

Course consistencyDoes this course connect logically to your previous education? A Bachelor of Nursing after completing high school — clear. A Master of IT after a Bachelor of Commerce with no IT units — needs explanation. The bigger the gap, the stronger the explanation needs to be.
Provider choiceWhy this specific university or TAFE? Generic answers like "it has a good reputation" are weak. Name specific programs, faculty, research groups, industry connections, or practical placements that are specific to that institution.
Why AustraliaYou must address why Australia over your home country or other destinations. Accepted reasons: specific qualifications not available locally, recognised credentials for international careers, specific industry exposure, English-medium instruction, work placement opportunities.
Understanding of course requirementsShow you know what you are signing up for. Mention the AQF level, duration, key subjects, assessment format, and any practical components. This demonstrates you have researched it genuinely, not just applied to get a visa.
3. How the course benefits you

DHA assesses whether the qualification makes career sense — whether completing it will genuinely improve your employment prospects or advance your professional development.

Career progressionShow a clear line from where you are now to where this course takes you. Not "it will help my career" but specifically: "I currently work as X, this course will qualify me for Y, which pays Z and is in growing demand in my country because W."
Relevance to home country employmentDHA considers whether the qualification is relevant to employment in your home country or another country. The course doesn't have to guarantee a job at home, but there should be a plausible connection.
Course level appropriatenessThe course should be consistent with your current education level. A significant downgrade — like a Masters graduate applying for a Certificate III — needs strong justification. AQF level jumps also need explanation.
Post-study pathwayUnder GS, you can mention the 485 post-study work visa as part of your plan. You can be honest that you want to gain Australian work experience after graduating. This is not held against you.
4. Immigration history

DHA reviews your full visa and travel history for Australia and other countries. A clean history is a strong foundation. Past issues need to be addressed proactively.

Previous Australian visasIf you have held a student visa before, there is an additional GS question. You must explain your complete study history in Australia, any course changes, any study gaps over 2 months, and the reasons for each.
Previous refusalsAny previous visa refusal — for Australia or any other country — must be declared. Failure to declare is PIC 4020 fraud and is a ground for refusal and possible 3-year ban. Declare everything and provide context.
Visa compliance historyIf you have held visas in Australia or other countries, DHA checks you complied with conditions. Overstays, work hour breaches, and condition violations all count against you.
Travel historyExtensive travel to multiple countries without obvious reason can raise questions. Travel for tourism or family visits is fine — just be prepared to explain it if asked.
5. Any other relevant information

This is the catch-all field. Use it to address anything that might look unusual in your application, or to add context that strengthens your case.

Political or civil unrestIf there is significant political instability, civil conflict, or mandatory military service in your home country, DHA wants you to address it. Acknowledge it and explain how it affects your circumstances without undermining your ties argument.
Health or family circumstancesAny unusual personal circumstances that explain your study decision should go here. Keep it factual and evidence-supported.
Gaps in study or employmentIf there are unexplained gaps in your academic or employment history, address them here. A 2-year gap with no explanation raises more questions than a 2-year gap with a clear reason.
Financial source explanationIf your funding source needs context — a family gift, sale of property, business proceeds — explain it here and attach supporting documentation.
Section 3

How to write your GS statement — with examples

DHA limits each GS answer to 150 words. This is tight. Every word must work. Below are the four main questions with a weak answer and a strong answer for each, showing exactly what the difference looks like.

Question 1 — Your current circumstances and ties to home country
DHA asks: Give details of your current circumstances including ties to family, community, employment and economic circumstances.
I have strong ties to my home country. My family lives there and I plan to return after completing my studies. I have a stable financial situation and am supported by my parents.
Why this fails: Vague, unspecific, reads like a template. No names, no specifics, no evidence of genuine ties.
I am currently employed as an Accounts Assistant at XYZ Company in Karachi (3 years). My parents and two siblings reside in Karachi — my father operates a small manufacturing business where I assist part-time. I own a motorbike and share a family property. My employer has confirmed my position will be held during my studies. I have been assessed for the AUD $29,710 living cost threshold via 12 months of bank statements showing consistent savings from my salary.
Why this works: Specific employer, specific family details, specific financial evidence, specific property. Nothing generic.
Question 2 — Why this course with this provider in Australia
DHA asks: Explain why you wish to study this course in Australia with this particular education provider. Include your understanding of the requirements of the course and of studying and living in Australia.
I chose this university because it has a good reputation internationally. The course will help me develop skills in my field. I researched Australia and believe it offers a good quality of life and excellent education standards.
Why this fails: This could describe any university in any country. Zero specificity. Extremely common refusal.
I chose the University of South Australia's Master of Nursing Practice because it is ANMAC-accredited, which is the specific body required for registration as a Registered Nurse in Australia. The program includes 800 hours of clinical placement — a practical component unavailable in equivalent programs in Pakistan. USQ's partnership with SA Health provides placement access I cannot access elsewhere. The course runs 2 years full-time, assessed via clinical competency and written examination. I have $35,000 AUD in savings covering tuition ($28,000) and living costs ($29,710 per DHA threshold). I understand the 48hr/fortnight work limit during sessions.
Why this works: Names the institution and course. Explains a specific accreditation reason. Mentions clinical hours, financial capacity, and demonstrates knowledge of visa conditions.
Question 3 — How completing the course will benefit you
DHA asks: Explain how completing the course will be of benefit to the applicant.
Completing this course will improve my employment prospects and help me advance in my career. It will open doors in the nursing field both in Australia and internationally.
Why this fails: Generic. No specifics about what the qualification actually achieves. DHA has seen this sentence thousands of times.
Pakistan faces a critical nursing shortage — the WHO reports a deficit of over 200,000 nurses. My Bachelor of Nursing (Pakistan) qualifies me to work as a Staff Nurse but not as a Registered Nurse at senior or specialised levels. The Australian ANMAC-assessed Master of Nursing Practice would qualify me as an RN at AQF Level 9, enabling senior ward management roles in Pakistan paying 40-60% above my current salary. I intend to gain 1-2 years of Australian clinical experience after the 485 post-study visa, then return to Pakistan to a supervisory nursing role at Aga Khan University Hospital, where I have a conditional offer pending ANMAC registration.
Why this works: Uses real data (WHO shortage), explains the specific career gap the course closes, mentions realistic salary improvement, and addresses the 485 honestly — which is allowed under GS.
Question 4 — Any other relevant information
DHA asks: Give details of any other relevant information the applicant would like to include.
I am a genuine student and look forward to studying in Australia. I will comply with all visa conditions and return to Pakistan after completing my studies.
Why this fails: Completely hollow. Saying you are genuine does not make you genuine. DHA knows this.
I note that a similar nursing master's program exists at Aga Khan University, Karachi. However, it is not ANMAC-accredited, meaning its graduates cannot access AHPRA registration without reassessment. This accreditation gap is the primary reason for studying in Australia rather than locally. I have no previous visa refusals in any country. I have held a UAE visit visa twice (2019, 2022) and complied fully with conditions. My 12-month bank statements (attached) show consistent deposits from my employer — there are no large unexplained transfers. Funds are not borrowed. My employer reference letter (attached) confirms my position will be retained during leave of absence.
Why this works: Proactively explains why local course is insufficient. Declares clean travel history. Addresses the funds source proactively — a common EL3 concern.
Section 4

Evidence Level 3 — what it means for your application

From 8 January 2026, DHA moved India, Nepal, Bangladesh and Bhutan to Evidence Level 3 (EL3) under the Simplified Student Visa Framework. This is the highest evidence level — meaning enhanced scrutiny, manual verification, and longer processing times. EL3 does not mean automatic refusal.

What EL3 actually requires — the full checklist
  • 3 consecutive months of bank statements showing consistent balance — not a single deposit made just before application
  • Source of funds documentation — salary slips, property sale contract, business income, family gift letter with statutory declaration
  • Bank statements authenticated by the issuing bank — DHA may call your bank directly to verify
  • Academic transcripts authenticated by the issuing institution — not just certified copies
  • NAATI-certified English translations for all non-English documents
  • Detailed GS statement addressing all five assessment areas — generic answers are insufficient at EL3
  • Police clearance from every country lived in for 12+ months in the past 10 years
  • Health examination completed before lodgement (front-loading speeds processing)
  • Apply at least 16 weeks before your course commencement date — not 8 weeks
  • Large unexplained deposits in the weeks before application raise red flags at EL3
  • Do not use borrowed funds presented as your own — DHA may verify directly with the lender
  • Do not use a generic GS statement template — EL3 officers look specifically for templated responses
EL3 does not mean automatic refusal. Well-prepared genuine applicants from India, Nepal, Bangladesh and Bhutan are approved every week. The difference between approval and refusal at EL3 is almost always documentation quality and GS statement specificity — not the country itself.
EL3 is not applied retrospectively. If your visa was lodged before 8 January 2026, the EL3 change does not affect your application in progress. EL3 applies at the time of lodgement only.
Section 5

The 8 most common GS refusal reasons

1

Generic or templated GS statement

A statement that could apply to any student is the single most common refusal ground. Phrases like "Australia offers world-class education" or "I am a genuine student who will return home" appear in thousands of applications. DHA knows what a template looks like. Every sentence must be specific to you, your course, and your institution.

2

No explanation for why Australia over home country

If an equivalent course exists in your home country and you don't explain why you're coming to Australia, DHA assumes the course is a pretext for the visa. You must identify the specific gap — accreditation, practical placement, English medium, clinical exposure — that your local option doesn't provide.

3

Course inconsistent with educational background

A Commerce graduate applying for a Master of Environmental Science with no explanation creates a logical gap DHA will question. Any significant course change or direction shift needs explicit explanation — ideally supported by evidence of relevant work experience, short courses, or clear career reasoning.

4

Funds that don't meet the AUD $29,710 living cost threshold

As of 2026, DHA requires evidence of AUD $29,710 per year for living costs, plus tuition and return airfare. Funds must be genuine, traceable, and available — not a single large deposit made days before application. For EL3 countries, 3 months of consistent bank history is required.

5

Undeclared previous visa refusals — PIC 4020 fraud

Failing to declare a previous visa refusal — for Australia or any other country — is treated as a fraudulent application under PIC 4020. This can result in a 3-year ban from applying. Declare everything and provide context. A refusal with a good explanation is far better than a hidden refusal discovered by DHA.

6

Weak ties to home country

A single person with no family, no employment, no property, and no community connections in their home country gives DHA little reason to believe they will maintain compliance. You need to demonstrate genuine ties — even if imperfect. A job offer, family responsibilities, property, a business, or professional licences that only operate at home all help.

7

Unexplained study gaps or course changes in Australia (returning students)

If you have previously studied in Australia, DHA examines your complete study record. Gaps of more than 2 months during an academic year, unexplained course changes, and poor academic progress all need explanation. Returning students face additional scrutiny under the separate GS question for prior student visa holders.

8

English below minimum — 150 word answers not in English

All GS responses must be in English. DHA does not accept answers in other languages, even for EL3 nationalities. If your English is weak, the GS answers themselves become evidence against you — since studying in an English-medium course requires sufficient English to participate. Your answers are also an implicit English test.

Section 7

Supporting documents — what to attach

DHA gives more weight to GS statements supported by evidence. The statement alone is insufficient — you must attach documents that verify what you claim. Here is what to prepare for each assessment area.

Previous studyAcademic transcripts (certified) · Degree certificates · Names of all institutions and dates attended · Any certificates of completion for short courses
Previous study in AustraliaComplete study record including all providers · Reasons for any course changes in writing · Academic progress reports · Any letters from institutions
Current employmentEmployment contract or letter from employer on company letterhead · Payslips for last 3–6 months · Position description · Contact details of supervisor who can confirm employment
Family tiesBirth certificates (parents, siblings) · Marriage certificate if married · Evidence of children (birth certificates, school enrolment) · Statutory declaration from family members if needed
Financial capacity3+ months of consecutive bank statements with transaction history · Source of funds documents (salary slips, tax returns, business income, property sale) · For EL3: bank authentication letter · Education loan approval (if applicable) · Sponsor financial documents with statutory declaration
Property / assetsProperty title documents · Land ownership certificates · Investment account statements · Business registration and financial statements
Immigration historyPrevious visa grant notices · Travel history summary · Any previous refusal letters (with your explanation) · Passport copies showing entry/exit stamps
Why AustraliaEvidence the course is not available locally or doesn't meet your need (e.g. institution website, accreditation comparison) · Industry or employer requirements showing Australian qualification is needed
Translation requirement: All non-English documents must be translated by a NAATI-certified translator or a DHA-approved translator. Other translations are not accepted.

Front-loading speeds processing: Complete health examination and police clearances before lodging your application. Applications with all supporting documents at lodgement process significantly faster than those waiting on document requests.
What happens after you lodge
  • DHA acknowledges receipt and assigns a Transaction Reference Number (TRN) — keep this
  • For EL3 nationalities: DHA may contact your bank directly to verify statement authenticity
  • For EL3 nationalities: DHA may call your education provider to verify your enrolment and CoE
  • DHA may request additional documents (Section 56 request) — respond within the timeframe given
  • If health exam or police clearance is outstanding, DHA will put your application on hold
  • You may be asked to attend an interview — rare but possible for complex cases
  • Decision issued via ImmiAccount — check regularly
  • Do not travel offshore while awaiting a decision without a Bridging Visa B — you may not be able to return
Section 9

Common questions answered

Can I mention PR plans in my GS statement? +
Yes. Under the Genuine Student requirement, future intentions to apply for permanent residency explicitly do not count against you. Ministerial Direction No. 106 states that "the GS requirement recognises that genuine students may develop skills Australia needs and may later choose to apply for permanent residence." You can honestly mention wanting a 485 post-study work visa, wanting to gain Australian work experience, or hoping to eventually explore PR pathways. What matters is whether your study intent is genuine — not your long-term migration aspirations.
How long is the GS statement? +
Each GS question in the ImmiAccount online form has a 150-word limit. There are typically 4–5 questions depending on your circumstances (there is an additional question if you have previously held a student visa or are applying from a non-student visa). Total across all questions: approximately 600–750 words. DHA strongly prefers that you answer directly in the form rather than attaching a separate document. If you attach a statement as well, the form answers take priority.
Do I need to attach a separate GS statement document? +
No. DHA explicitly states they prefer applicants to respond to the GS questions in the application form rather than attaching a separate statement. You should answer all questions directly in the form. You can attach supporting documents (bank statements, employment letters, transcripts) — but the written GS answers themselves should go in the form, not in an attached PDF.
What is the difference between GTE and GS? +
The Genuine Temporary Entrant (GTE) requirement was replaced by the Genuine Student (GS) requirement on 23 March 2024. Applications lodged before that date continue to be assessed under GTE. The key practical difference is that GS explicitly acknowledges that genuine students may later apply for permanent residency — this is not held against you under GS as it could be under GTE. GS also changed the format: answers go directly into the ImmiAccount form with a 150-word limit per question, whereas GTE was typically a separate attached document with no fixed word limit.
My country is now EL3. Does that mean I will be refused? +
No. EL3 means enhanced scrutiny — not automatic refusal. Well-prepared genuine applicants from India, Nepal, Bangladesh and Bhutan continue to be approved regularly. The change means DHA will verify your financial documents more thoroughly, may contact your bank and institution directly, and will scrutinise your GS statement more carefully. The solution is thorough documentation, a specific and non-generic GS statement, and applying 16+ weeks before your course commencement.
I have a previous visa refusal. Do I have to declare it? +
Yes — always. Failure to declare a previous visa refusal (for Australia or any other country) constitutes fraud under Public Interest Criterion 4020 (PIC 4020). If discovered, this can result in your application being refused and a 3-year ban from applying for most Australian visas. A declared refusal with a clear explanation is far better than an undisclosed refusal. Explain the circumstances, what has changed since, and why the concern no longer applies.
A similar course is available in my home country. What do I do? +
You must address this directly in your GS statement. DHA specifically considers "reasons for not studying in their home country or region if a similar course is available there." Accepted explanations include: the local version is not accredited by the relevant Australian body (e.g. ANMAC, AHPRA, Engineers Australia); the local version does not include the practical components your career requires; the Australian qualification is specifically required by your employer or industry; or English-medium instruction is necessary for your international career goals. Be specific — a general claim that "Australia is better" is not sufficient.
Can I apply for a student visa from inside Australia on a different visa? +
It depends on your current visa. Holders of certain visas cannot apply for a student visa while in Australia, including Visitor visas, Temporary Graduate (485) visas, and Maritime Crew visas. If you are currently on a student visa, you can apply for a further student visa to extend your studies. If you are on another visa (e.g. working holiday, bridging visa), check the DHA Visa Finder tool to confirm whether an onshore application is permitted. There is an additional GS question for applicants lodging from a non-student visa in Australia.
What is the financial requirement for the student visa in 2026? +
As of 2026, DHA requires evidence that you can meet: AUD $29,710 per year for living costs (main applicant), plus full tuition fees for your course, plus return airfare costs. For a partner included in your application: additional living costs per DHA tables. For dependent children: additional amounts per child. Funds must be genuine, traceable, and available — not borrowed funds presented as your own, and not a single large deposit made days before application. For EL3 nationalities (India, Nepal, Bangladesh, Bhutan), 3 months of consistent bank history is required showing the source of funds.
